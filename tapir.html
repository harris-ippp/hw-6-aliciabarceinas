Baird's tapir is the largest native mammal in the neotropics

About the size of small donkeys, Baird’s tapirs (Tapirus bairdii) typically weigh between 150 to 300 kilograms. They are dark brown to reddish brown above and paler below with white fringes around their large ears, white lips, and sometimes a white patch on the throat and chest

Tapirs' closets relatives are rhinoceroses and horses, whith whom they share having an odd number of toes (Perissodactyla: perissós, “uneven” + dáktulos, “a finger, toe”)

The Baird's Tapir is known to occur in Southern Mexico, Belize, Costa Rica, Guatemala, southern Mexico, Honduras, Nicaragua, Republic of Panama, north-western Colombia, and historically in El Salvador. Its current or historical presence is still uncertain in Ecuador You can find a map of this species distribution here.

In some habitats, Baird’s tapirs are important seed dispersers. The tapir's role in dispersing seeds was demonstrated by a study in Ecuador, which showed that vascular plants eaten by the mountain tapir germinated in its feces, as you can see here.

It has also been suggested that T. bairdii is an important species for indicating the overall health of Neotropical rain forests because of their rarity and sensitivity to disturbance. However, habitat loss and hunting has made the populations decline more than 50% in the past years, and therefore it is classified as 'Endangered' by the UICN. It is estimated that around 70% of Central American forest areas have been lost through deforestation and alteration over the last 40 years.
